2 Introduction

The plots and tables presented are part of a systematic literature review and meta-analysis of influenza vaccine effectiveness studies using the test-negative design.

Meta-analysed pooled estimates are provided separately for each age category, and within age categories separate estimates are shown for each season.

2.1 Selection of studies

Papers were identified for this review as follows:

  1. A search was performed in Medline and Embase using search terms “influenza” or “flu”, “vaccine”, “efficacy”, “effectiveness”, “test-negative design”, “case-control” and related terms.

  2. Papers were included if they reported VE estimates against any influenza (types A or B) using the test-negative design.

  3. Only “absolute VE” estimates are considered where the comparison group are unvaccinated individuals; studies that compared different vaccines were not included.

2.2 Selection of estimates for meta-analysis

  1. VE estimates have been extracted for each age group, setting (inpatient or outpatient) and vaccine reported.

  2. Where estimates were reported using data that has already been published (e.g. re-analyses), the original report was included unless an estimate in the new study was reported for a previously-unpublished age category or setting.

  3. Where data were reported in site or country-specific analyses and in a pooled analysis (e.g. pooled across Europe), the estimates from the larger study were included. For example, if estimates were reported by Spain using data that also contributed to I-MOVE the estimates reported by I-MOVE have been used. It is possible, however, that some estimates from the Spainsh study may be used if they were not also reported by the I-MVE collaboration, such as VE for a specific vaccine or age group.

  4. Where sensitivity analyses were performed, estimates from the main analysis were included. This is usually the analysis reported in teh abstract. An exception to this rule was studies that excluded SARS-CoV-2 positive controls, in which case the analysis that excluded these controls was preferentially selected to reduce the potential for confounding. For studies that estimated VE against different control groups (e.g. pan-negative, influenza-negative, other-respiratory virus-positive), the estimate that considered controls as any patient testing negative for influenza was used.

  5. Meta-estimates are only provided when at least three studies were eligible for inclusion in a particular subgroup analysis.

2.3 Additional notes for subgroup analyses:

2.3.1 Age group

All estimates are provided by age group

  1. Estimates for the broadest relevant age range for an age category were selected. For example, when considering VE for children aged <5years, if a study reported estimates for children 6m-<2y, 2-<5y and 6m-5y the 6m-5y estimate is used. If the study had not reported VE for 6m-<5y then both the 6m-<2y and 2-<5y estimates are used.

2.3.2 Vaccine type

Subgroup analyses are included for different vaccine types

  1. Where a study has reported VE for any vaccine type and for specific vaccine types, the estimate for any vaccine is used in the main meta-analysis. For example, if a study reported estimates for inactivated influenza vaccines (IIV) and live attenuated vaccines (LAIV) and both vaccines combined, the combined estimate is used in the main analysis.
  2. For vaccine-type specific estimates, the estimates for LAIV and IIV would be used for the LAIV and the IIV summary meta-estimates.

2.3.3 Patient setting

Subgroup analysis are included for different patient settings

  1. For the main analysis, estimates from both inpatient and outpatient settings are used. If a study reported separate estimates for inpatients and outpatients as well as an estimate combining both patient types, the combined estimate was used; otherwise the two separate estimates were included.
  2. For setting-specific estimates, only the estimates that were specific to inpatients or outpatients were used and these were not combined in meta-analysis.
  3. Studies that recruited from urgent care and emergency departments are typically classified as outpatient. However, if the study was set in a hospital and included patients from any ward including patients admitted to the emergency ward, then the estimates are considered inpatient estimates.
